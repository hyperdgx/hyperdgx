## `01` // Identity

Most GitHub profiles tell you what someone knows.

This one tells you what drives me.

I'm **Harsh Pawar** — Digital Product Designer, Frontend Developer, and compulsive problem-solver from **Bhopal, India**. Online, I go by **[HyperDGX](https://github.com/hyperdgx)**.

I build digital products at the intersection of **product strategy, user experience, visual design, and frontend engineering** — not as separate disciplines you hand off between siloed teams, but as one continuous craft I carry from the first user question to the last commit.

The word that defines me is **curiosity**. Not as a personality trait — as a discipline. Curiosity is what pushed me into design before I understood HTML. Into product thinking before I understood business. Into building full-scale platforms before I was certain I had the skills to finish them. It's what makes me ask *why does this exist?* before I ever ask *how should this look?*

I don't separate design from development anymore. The best product work happens when the person making the design decisions understands the medium those decisions will live in. That gap — between what was designed and what got built — is where products fall apart. I close that gap.

If you're building something and you care about craft — the design, the code, and the thinking behind both — let's work together.

## `03` // What I Actually Do

A skill list tells you nothing real. Here's what each discipline means in practice.

<details>
<summary><strong>Product Design &amp; UX Research</strong></summary>

<br>

Before I open Figma, I ask: *What problem are we solving? Who is this for? What does failure look like?*

UX work for me means user research, information architecture, friction mapping, user journey design, and usability validation — not just wireframes to hand to a developer. I design flows that reduce cognitive load, eliminate unnecessary decisions, and guide users to their goals without them noticing the effort it took to get them there.

I believe a well-designed product is one where the interface disappears and the experience is all that remains.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Interface Design (UI)</strong></summary>

<br>

Visual design isn't decoration — it's communication. Every typographic decision creates hierarchy. Every color choice carries meaning. Every spacing decision creates rhythm or chaos.

I build interfaces with systematic thinking: design tokens, typography scales, component states (default, hover, active, disabled, error, loading), dark/light mode systems, responsive breakpoints, and micro-interaction design. The goal is always an interface that feels *inevitable* — like it couldn't have been built any other way.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Frontend Engineering</strong></summary>

<br>

I translate designs into production-ready interfaces that perform. React, Next.js, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, GSAP scroll animations, Lenis smooth scroll, SplitType kinetic typography — I work across the full visual engineering layer.

I care about Core Web Vitals (LCP, INP, CLS), accessibility (WCAG 2.2), semantic HTML, component architecture patterns, and code that remains readable and maintainable two years after the first commit. The frontend isn't where design gets "implemented" — it's where it either works or it doesn't.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Design Systems</strong></summary>

<br>

Inconsistency doesn't just look bad — it costs teams real time and real money at scale. I build design systems that create lasting coherence: design tokens for color, spacing, typography, and elevation; reusable component libraries built for both design and code; documentation that developers actually use; and handoff standards that survive the original designer leaving.

A good design system doesn't constrain product teams. It gives them leverage.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Full-Stack Development</strong></summary>

<br>

When the product demands it, I go deep into the stack. Django for backend architecture. Django Channels for real-time WebSocket features. PostgreSQL with Supabase for multi-schema data management. Celery and Redis for background task queues. Cloudinary for asset pipelines. Groq for AI integration. JWT for authentication. Node.js and Express for admin tooling and CMS systems.

I'm not just a frontend developer who touched a backend once. I've built production systems that real users depend on.

</details>

## `13` // How I Actually Work

Worth knowing before we collaborate:

**I start with questions, not deliverables.** Before opening Figma, I want to understand the user, the problem, and what failure looks like. A brief that can't answer those questions isn't ready to design yet.

**I believe in one great thing over ten average things.** I'd rather build one section that's genuinely excellent than ten sections that are adequately fine. Quality is a decision you make before you start, not a polish phase at the end.

**I document my decisions.** Every meaningful design choice has a reason. Every architectural decision has a reason. I write them down. Future collaborators — and future me — need to know *why*, not just *what*.

**I push back when something's wrong.** If a design decision doesn't serve the user or the business goal, I'll say so — with reasoning, not just opinion. I'm not building a portfolio of things I agreed to. I'm building things that work.

**I'm uncomfortable with vague briefs.** The more clarity we establish upfront, the better the output. Ambiguity isn't a design opportunity; it's a risk that compounds.

**I test in real browsers.** Figma and Chrome are different environments. What looks perfect in a design file and what actually performs in production are not the same thing. I bridge that gap.

**I believe iteration beats perfection — but only when the foundation is solid.** Move fast on the details. Move carefully on the architecture.
